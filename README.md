# relQC4QC
relativistic quantum chemistry for quantum computers

This research is essentially where Einstein's relativity meets the weird world of quantum computing. Instead of just calculating basic chemistry, you’re looking at atoms so heavy that their electrons move at speeds where classical physics just gives up.

By pulling data from DIRAC and translating it into Qiskit Nature, you’re building a bridge between high-energy physics and actual qubits.

## 🚀 The Mission: Relativistic Quantum Chemistry

Most quantum simulations ignore relativity to save on "math homework." But for your molecules, that’s not an option. You’re mapping the Relativistic Hamiltonian—which accounts for things like spin-orbit coupling—directly onto a quantum processor to see how these atoms actually "hug" or "repel" each other.

## 🧪 The Molecular Lineup

These aren't just random chemicals; they are the "celebrities" of the quantum hardware world:

    Rb2​ (Rubidium Dimer): The backbone of ultracold physics. It’s a primary candidate for quantum memory because it can be controlled with extreme precision using lasers.

    RbH (Rubidium Hydride): A molecule with a dipole moment. This makes it perfect for simulating complex materials where particles need to interact over long distances.

    SbH (Antimony Hydride): The heavy hitter. Antimony is so dense that relativistic effects aren't just a "tweak"—they define the molecule’s entire existence.
